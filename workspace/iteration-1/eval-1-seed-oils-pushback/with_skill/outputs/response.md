Short answer: no, it's not compelling once you look at the actual data. The "seed oils are the main cause of chronic disease" claim is popular online but it is not where the human evidence points. Let me show the work.

## Claims you're making

1. Seed oils (canola, soybean, sunflower, etc.) are inflammatory in humans.
2. They are "basically the main cause" of chronic disease in the West.
3. Olive oil is better than seed oils.
4. Butter is better than seed oils.

These are four separate claims and they have very different levels of support.

## Evidence, per claim

**1. "Seed oils are inflammatory."**
This is the central mechanistic claim — usually framed as: linoleic acid (the main omega-6 in seed oils) converts to arachidonic acid, which produces pro-inflammatory eicosanoids.

The biochemistry is real. The human evidence that it raises inflammatory markers is not. A 2012 systematic review of controlled feeding trials by Johnson and Fritsche (Journal of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics) found essentially no effect of dietary linoleic acid on circulating inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6, TNF-α, etc.) in healthy adults. More recent work — including a 2025 analysis from the University at Buffalo / UB led by Maki and colleagues, reported in the lay press as the "myth-busting" study — found higher plasma linoleic acid was associated with *lower* inflammation and better cardiometabolic markers, not higher.

Evidence rung: replicated RCTs + recent cohort data converging. The "inflammatory" claim is at rung 7 (mechanistic reasoning) dressed up as rung 1. The human data does not support it.

**2. "Main cause of chronic disease in the West."**
This is the strongest form of the claim and it has essentially no empirical support. Chronic disease in the West tracks most strongly with total calories / obesity, smoking history, physical inactivity, alcohol, refined carbohydrates and added sugar, ultra-processed food intake as a category, and socioeconomic factors. Seed oil intake rose alongside many of these, which is where the correlation-as-causation intuition comes from — but in the controlled data, swapping saturated fat for polyunsaturated fat *reduces* cardiovascular events (Hooper et al., Cochrane review on reducing saturated fat intake, updated 2020; Mozaffarian, Micha & Wallace meta-analysis, PLoS Medicine 2010).

Calling seed oils "the main cause" of Western chronic disease is the single weakest part of your frame. I'd flag this as (a) confirmation bias and (b) correlation-as-causation.

**3. "Olive oil is better than seed oils."**
Partially defensible, with caveats.
- Extra virgin olive oil has the best outcome evidence of any cooking fat, largely because of the PREDIMED RCT (Estruch et al., NEJM 2013, republished 2018 after reanalysis): ~30% relative reduction in major cardiovascular events vs. a low-fat control in a high-risk Mediterranean population.
- But PREDIMED compared EVOO + Mediterranean diet to a *low-fat* diet, not to seed oils. There is no large RCT showing EVOO beats canola or soybean oil head-to-head on hard endpoints.
- On lipid panels, canola and safflower oil actually outperform olive oil for LDL reduction in network meta-analyses (Schwingshackl et al., Advances in Nutrition 2018).

So: EVOO is excellent. "Much better than seed oils" is overstated.

**4. "Butter is better than seed oils."**
This one is backwards relative to the current best data.
- Zhang et al., JAMA Internal Medicine, March 2025 — pooled Nurses' Health Study I/II and Health Professionals Follow-up Study, ~221,000 adults, 33 years of follow-up. Highest quartile of butter intake: ~15% higher total mortality. Highest quartile of plant-based oil intake (including soybean, canola, olive): ~16% lower total mortality. Substituting 10g/day of butter with plant oils: ~17% lower total mortality.
- This is observational, so confounding is possible — but it converges with RCT-level evidence that replacing saturated fat with polyunsaturated fat lowers CVD events (Hooper et al. Cochrane 2020; Mozaffarian et al. PLoS Med 2010).

The hierarchy for hard outcomes currently looks like: EVOO ≈ other plant oils > butter. Not the other way around.

## Steelman of the "seed oils are bad" view

The strongest version — which serious researchers actually argue — is not "seed oils cause chronic disease." It's narrower:

- Industrial seed oils are often consumed as part of ultra-processed foods (fried snacks, fast food, commercial baked goods). The *package* they arrive in is clearly harmful. Disentangling the oil from the processing is hard.
- Repeatedly heated frying oil produces aldehydes and oxidized lipids that are plausibly harmful (Grootveld et al. have published on this for years). A restaurant deep fryer is not the same exposure as canola oil in a home salad dressing.
- The omega-6 to omega-3 ratio in the modern Western diet has shifted dramatically, and while raising omega-3 intake is clearly beneficial, the specific claim that *lowering omega-6* independently helps is less clear.
- A minority of researchers (e.g., Chris Ramsden's reanalyses of the Sydney Diet Heart Study and Minnesota Coronary Experiment, BMJ 2013 and 2016) have argued the historical RCT evidence for replacing saturated fat with linoleic acid is weaker than commonly claimed.

That is the honest steelman. Note what it is not: it is not "seed oils are the main cause of chronic disease." The steelman is "industrial food containing seed oils is bad, and a couple of old trials may have been over-sold." That's a much narrower claim and I'd largely agree with it.

## Verdict

- "Seed oils are inflammatory in humans" — **not supported by controlled human data.**
- "Main cause of chronic disease in the West" — **not supported; this is the weakest part of the argument.**
- "Olive oil is better than seed oils" — **olive oil is excellent; 'much better' overstates the gap.**
- "Butter is better than seed oils" — **current best evidence points the other way.**

Calibrated position: the popular "seed oils are poison" narrative is mostly wrong on the science. What *is* defensible is avoiding ultra-processed foods (which happen to contain a lot of seed oils), not re-using deep-fryer oil, and eating more omega-3s. Those are real. They are also not the claim you actually made.

I'm flagging my own risk here: I'm pushing back hard, and I want to be clear it's because the evidence is genuinely asymmetric, not because contrarianism is the default.

## What would change my verdict

- A well-powered RCT with hard endpoints (CVD events, mortality) directly comparing refined seed oils vs. butter or vs. olive oil, showing seed oils worse.
- A meta-analysis of inflammatory-marker RCTs showing a consistent pro-inflammatory signal from dietary linoleic acid at normal intakes.
- A credible mechanistic account that explains why the observational mortality data (Zhang 2025, the NHS/HPFS pools) and the lipid RCTs both point the opposite direction from the "seed oils are bad" claim.
