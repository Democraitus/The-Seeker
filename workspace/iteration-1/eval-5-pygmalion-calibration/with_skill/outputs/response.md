This is a case where the popular version of the finding and the actual evidence have come apart. Let me separate them.

## Claims to unpack

You're making two claims that often travel together but deserve separate grading:

1. **Strong claim:** The Rosenthal & Jacobson (1968) "Pygmalion in the Classroom" study *proved* that teacher expectations substantially raise student IQ.
2. **Weaker claim:** Teacher expectations can influence student performance to some degree (the "Pygmalion effect" as a general phenomenon).

These get very different grades.

## Evidence

**On the original 1968 study — weak.**
Rosenthal & Jacobson reported that randomly labeled "bloomers" gained ~24.8 IQ points over controls. That specific finding has not held up well:

- Robert L. Thorndike's 1968 review in the *American Educational Research Journal* argued the TOGA IQ instrument used in the study was badly misapplied at the youngest grades — some pre-test scores were so low as to be implausible, which he said made the key results "worthless" as evidence for the headline claim.
- Richard Snow's critique ("Unfinished Pygmalion," *Contemporary Psychology*, 1969) raised similar concerns about measurement and analysis.
- Early replication attempts through the 1970s largely failed to reproduce the dramatic IQ gain. Elashoff & Snow's 1971 book-length reanalysis (*Pygmalion Reconsidered*) is the standard reference for this.

So the *specific* study you named does not, on its own, establish what it's famous for.

**On the broader phenomenon — real but small.**
The most cited modern synthesis is Jussim & Harber (2005), "Teacher Expectations and Self-Fulfilling Prophecies: Knowns and Unknowns, Resolved and Unresolved Controversies," *Personality and Social Psychology Review* 9(2), 131–155. Their review of ~35 years of work concluded, roughly:

- Self-fulfilling prophecy effects in classrooms are real but *typically small* (effect sizes around r ≈ .1–.3).
- Effects tend to dissipate rather than accumulate over time.
- A substantial portion of the correlation between teacher expectations and student outcomes appears to reflect *accurate* teacher perception, not self-fulfillment.
- Effects may be larger for students from stigmatized groups — but that is a narrower claim than the original.

This sits around rung 1–2 on the evidence hierarchy (a careful narrative review of many studies, including meta-analytic work), so it's reasonably strong for the modest version of the claim.

**Grade:**
- "Teacher expectations *proved* to change student IQ by 24 points" — **not supported.** That specific effect did not replicate.
- "Teacher expectations can have small, real effects on student performance, especially for marginalized students, usually short-lived" — **supported**, at moderate confidence.

## Steelman of the strong version

The best case for taking Rosenthal seriously: (a) his broader program on experimenter expectancy effects *did* replicate robustly outside the classroom, (b) a meaningful share of post-1968 classroom studies found *some* expectancy effect in the predicted direction (Rosenthal's own later meta-analyses made this case), and (c) it's genuinely hard to cleanly measure because teachers' expectations are partly *accurate*, which biases studies toward finding nothing causal. A defender would say the small effect sizes underestimate the real effect because of measurement noise. That is a real argument, not a crazy one. But it doesn't rescue the "IQ jumps 24 points" claim — it rescues the modest version.

## Verdict

Calibrated answer: **"Well-established" overstates it, and "the Rosenthal & Jacobson study proved it" is wrong.** The 1968 study is historically influential but methodologically shaky, and its headline finding did not replicate. The general phenomenon — that teacher expectations have some causal effect on student outcomes — is supported by later work, but the effects are small, often fade, and a chunk of the raw correlation reflects accurate perception rather than prophecy.

The honest shorthand: *small real effect, large mythologized effect.* The version of the Pygmalion effect that lives in pop-psychology books, TED talks, and management training is much stronger than anything the data supports.

## What would change my mind

- A well-powered, pre-registered RCT with random assignment of teacher expectations and objective outcome measures showing large, durable effects on achievement or IQ.
- A recent meta-analysis (post-2015, ideally registered) with low heterogeneity showing effect sizes substantially larger than Jussim & Harber's r ≈ .1–.3 range.
- A direct replication of the original Oak School study using modern IQ instruments that reproduces the ~24-point gap.

I am not aware of any of those existing as of my knowledge. If you have one, bring it and I'll update.
