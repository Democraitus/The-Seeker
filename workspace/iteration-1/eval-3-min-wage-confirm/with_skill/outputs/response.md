Short answer: yes, you are reading the mainstream empirical literature roughly correctly, but the claim needs to be stated precisely or it will smuggle in more than the evidence supports. Let me separate what's well-established from what's still contested, because "modest increases show small or null effects" is defensible while "minimum wage increases don't cost jobs" is stronger than the evidence.

## Claims

What you are actually asserting, as I read it:
- (Factual) The empirical literature on *modest* minimum wage increases in the US (and similar economies) finds employment effects that are small or statistically indistinguishable from zero.
- (Implicit) The "massive unemployment" claim you see online is not representative of what the careful empirical work shows.

Note what you are *not* claiming, which is important: you are not saying minimum wages can never cost jobs, and you are not making a claim about very large increases or increases that push the minimum far above the local median wage.

## Evidence

**Rung 1-3 (meta-analyses, quasi-experiments, strong designs):**

- Cengiz, Dube, Lindner, and Zipperer (2019), *QJE* — uses a "bunching" estimator on 138 state-level US minimum wage changes from 1979-2016. Finds that the number of jobs paying below the new minimum falls by roughly the same amount that jobs at and slightly above the minimum rise, so the net change in low-wage employment is close to zero over the five years following the increase. This is probably the single most-cited recent piece of evidence for the "small or null" view. ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25434), [QJE](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/134/3/1405/5484905))

- Dube (2019), *Impacts of Minimum Wages: Review of the International Evidence*, commissioned by HM Treasury / Low Pay Commission (UK). A systematic review of the modern international literature. Conclusion: "the most up to date body of research... points to a very muted effect of minimum wages on employment, while significantly increasing the earnings of low paid workers." ([GOV.UK PDF](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/government/uploads/system/uploads/attachment_data/file/844350/impacts_of_minimum_wages_review_of_the_international_evidence_Arindrajit_Dube_web.pdf))

- Card and Krueger (1994), *AER* — the classic New Jersey vs. Pennsylvania fast-food study that kicked off the modern literature. Found no disemployment from NJ's 1992 increase. Methodologically contested for years (Neumark & Wascher re-ran it with payroll data and got different results), but the broader program of quasi-experimental work that followed has largely sided with the small/null finding for moderate increases. ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w4509))

**Rung 3-5 (the dissenting strand):**

- Neumark and Wascher have spent decades arguing the opposite: that the preponderance of credible studies does show negative employment effects, especially for less-skilled workers. Their 2007 NBER review (w12663) is the standard reference for this position. You should not dismiss this strand — these are serious labor economists with real data, not ideologues. ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w12663))

- Jardim et al. (Seattle Minimum Wage Study, NBER 23532, later published in *AEJ: Economic Policy* 2022) — studied Seattle's increase from $9.47 toward $13. Found the second-phase increase reduced hours worked in low-wage jobs by roughly 6-7%, while raising hourly wages about 3%, implying a net earnings loss at the bottom. This is a well-identified study and it's a genuine counterexample. But note two things: Seattle's increase was *large*, pushing the minimum to a high share of the local median wage, and the study has methodological critics (EPI, Zipperer, others noted the exclusion of multi-site firms from the main estimate). ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w23532), [AEA](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pol.20180578))

## Steelman of the opposing view

The strongest version of "minimum wages cost jobs" is *not* the cartoon version you see online. It goes like this: the modern consensus of "small or null" effects comes from studies of *modest* increases (e.g., raising a minimum that's 35-45% of the local median wage by another few percent). The labor economics profession does *not* have strong evidence about large increases — say, pushing the minimum to 60-70% of the local median, or to a federal $15 in low-wage states where that approaches or exceeds the local median. Extrapolating "small effects" from modest interventions to large ones is exactly the out-of-sample inference the literature cannot support. Seattle, Puerto Rico (historic), and some European cases suggest disemployment effects *do* show up when the bite is large enough. Neumark also argues that aggregate employment hides the real effect, which falls disproportionately on teenagers and very low-skilled workers whose jobs are most exposed.

This version is defensible and partly correct. The honest response is: the literature is calibrated to the modest-increase case, and extrapolation is risky.

## Verdict

**On your specific reading: you are substantially right.** For the kinds of minimum wage increases that have actually been studied in the US and similar economies over the last 30 years — incremental increases that keep the minimum within the historical range relative to the median wage — the best-identified evidence (Cengiz et al., Dube's UK review, and the broader quasi-experimental literature) finds employment effects that are small, often statistically indistinguishable from zero, and accompanied by real wage gains for low-paid workers. "Massive unemployment" is not what the evidence shows for these policies.

**Caveats that matter:**
1. This is not a settled consensus in the sense that physics has one. Neumark, Wascher, and others still dissent with serious methods; they are a minority but not a fringe.
2. The finding is about *modest* increases. Extrapolating to a very large increase (e.g., doubling the minimum, or pushing it to 70%+ of the local median in a low-wage region) is not well-supported by the existing evidence in either direction.
3. Employment is not the only margin. Hours, non-wage benefits, scheduling, and price pass-through can all absorb minimum wage increases. "No employment effect" does not mean "no effect."
4. Regional variation matters. A $15 federal floor in Manhattan and a $15 federal floor in rural Mississippi are different policies, empirically.

So when you see someone online claim "massive unemployment": if they mean a modest increase, they are making a claim the recent empirical literature does not support. If they mean a very large increase in a low-wage region, the literature doesn't cleanly rule them out either — it just doesn't have the data.

## What would change my mind

- A pre-registered meta-analysis of the post-2015 $15 minimum wage experiments (California, New York, Seattle, etc.) showing consistent, replicated employment losses using credible designs would shift me toward Neumark's view.
- A successful replication of Cengiz et al. (2019) failing — i.e., finding the bunching estimator breaks down or that the "missing jobs" are in fact hidden disemployment — would significantly weaken the small/null position.
- Stronger evidence on the hours, scheduling, and benefit-substitution margins showing that the "no employment effect" finding is masking substantial compensation losses would matter for the underlying policy question even if it doesn't change the narrow employment claim.
