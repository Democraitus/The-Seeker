# On the minimum wage evidence

Short version up front: **your read is broadly correct for the claim you actually made** — that *modest* minimum wage increases, as studied in the modern US literature, produce small or null aggregate employment effects. But the "massive unemployment" framing and the "small/null effects" framing are not the only two options, and the honest picture has a third piece: there is real, not-dismissible evidence of negative effects concentrated on specific margins (hours rather than headcount; less-experienced workers; teens; very large increases relative to local wage medians). Smoothing that out would be the sycophantic answer. I want to actually lay it out.

## Claims I'm evaluating

1. (Factual) "Modest minimum wage increases cause massive unemployment" — the claim you're seeing made.
2. (Factual) "The empirical literature on modest increases shows small or null employment effects" — your read.
3. (Implicit) These are the only two positions; one must be right.

## Evidence, graded

**On claim 2 (your read) — strong support for the aggregate version:**

- **Cengiz, Dube, Lindner, and Zipperer (2019), *Quarterly Journal of Economics*** — Pooled 138 state minimum-wage changes in the US, 1979–2016, using a "bunching estimator" that counts jobs appearing just above the new floor and jobs disappearing just below it. Headline result: the number of jobs below the new minimum fell by roughly the same amount as jobs appeared at or above it, so the *net* low-wage job count was essentially unchanged over five years. Wages rose. This is a strong quasi-experimental design on a large sample — rung 3 on the evidence hierarchy, arguably the single most cited modern piece of evidence on the "small/null" side. ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25434), [QJE](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/134/3/1405/5484905))
- **Dube (2019), Review for the UK Low Pay Commission** — International evidence review. Conclusion: "the most up to date body of research from US, UK and other developed countries points to a very muted effect of minimum wages on employment, while significantly increasing the earnings of low paid workers." This review directly informed the UK's decision to keep pushing the National Living Wage toward two-thirds of median earnings. ([full report, gov.uk PDF](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/5dc0312940f0b637a03ffa96/impacts_of_minimum_wages_review_of_the_international_evidence_Arindrajit_Dube_web.pdf))
- **Card and Krueger (1994), *American Economic Review*** — The original New Jersey/Pennsylvania fast-food diff-in-diff after NJ's 1992 minimum went from $4.25 to $5.05. Found no employment reduction; if anything, NJ employment rose relative to PA. Methodologically contested for decades (payroll-records rematches narrowed but did not overturn the result), but it's the foundation stone of the "small effects" view. ([AER](https://ideas.repec.org/a/aea/aecrev/v84y1994i4p772-93.html), [Card PDF](https://davidcard.berkeley.edu/papers/njmin-aer.pdf))

**On claim 1 ("massive unemployment") — weak support for the strong version, but not zero support for a weaker version:**

The "massive unemployment" claim in its strong form is not what modern labor economists who dispute the null-effect view actually argue. Even the most skeptical recent work lands on modest-to-moderate disemployment, not catastrophic. But there is a serious dissenting literature that should not be dismissed.

## Steelman of the opposing view (the one that actually has evidence)

This is the part where I want to slow down rather than toss a one-line counterpoint. The steelman is not "minimum wages cause mass unemployment." The steelman is: **"The aggregate null masks real disemployment effects concentrated on specific populations and margins, and those effects get larger as the minimum wage gets higher relative to the local wage distribution."** Three pieces of actual evidence:

1. **Neumark and Shirley (2022), *Industrial Relations*** — "Myth or measurement." This is a systematic survey of the entire post-1992 US subnational-variation literature. Their finding: across all studies, there is "a clear preponderance of negative estimates"; the evidence of disemployment is *stronger* for teens, young adults, and the less-educated; it is *stronger still* in studies that look at directly affected workers rather than proxying with a low-wage industry like restaurants. Their headline stat: of the studies they surveyed, about 79% find negative employment effects, and among the subset they judge most credible the share is higher. ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w28388), [full PDF](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w28388/w28388.pdf), [Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/irel.12306))

   *This is not a fringe paper and I should not wave it off.* The Cengiz et al. bunching result and the Neumark–Shirley narrative review are both serious, and they genuinely disagree — not on the raw numbers, but on how to weight studies and how to read the identification strategies. Anyone telling you "the literature is settled" is picking a side.

2. **Clemens and Wither (2019), *Journal of Public Economics*** — Used the SIPP to track the same low-skilled individuals through the 2007–2009 federal minimum wage increases (to $7.25), comparing "bound" states (where the federal increase raised the floor) to "unbound" states (where state minima already exceeded it). Found targeted individuals' employment rates fell by 6.6 percentage points — about 9% — more in bound than unbound states by the second year. This is a large estimate, and it's for a period when the labor market was weak (important: disemployment effects may be state-dependent on macro conditions). ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w20724), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0047272719300052))

3. **Jardim, Long, Plotnick, van Inwegen, Vigdor, and Wething (2022), *American Economic Journal: Economic Policy*** — Seattle's minimum raised to $11 (2015) and $13 (2016). Using Washington State administrative payroll data (which, unusually, records hours), they find:
   - Headcount employment in low-wage jobs: ambiguous / modestly negative.
   - **Hours** in low-wage jobs: clearly down — roughly 6–7% fewer hours after the $13 step.
   - Total low-wage payroll: *fell*, implying workers lost more in hours than they gained in the hourly rate — an average loss of around $74/month per low-wage job at the $13 step, in their main specification.
   - Distributional finding: experienced workers gained; workers with no prior Washington work experience saw reduced hiring and no earnings gain.

   This is a rung-3 quasi-experimental design on high-quality administrative data, and it's the strongest single piece of adversarial evidence on *modern, moderate* (not outlandish) minimum wage increases. ([AEA](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pol.20180578), [NBER 2017 original](https://www.nber.org/papers/w23532), [NBER 2018 individual trajectories](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25182))

   Caveat the other direction: the Seattle study has been seriously critiqued — EPI and the Berkeley IRLE argued the data excluded multi-site firms (dropping chains like McDonald's), which could bias toward finding hours reductions in the surviving single-site sample. Jardim et al. addressed some but not all of those objections in the published 2022 version. So this is contested, but it is not refuted. ([EPI critique](https://www.epi.org/publication/the-high-road-seattle-labor-market-and-the-effects-of-the-minimum-wage-increase-data-limitations-and-methodological-problems-bias-new-analysis-of-seattles-minimum-wage-incr/))

4. **CBO (2019, updated 2021)** — Not a study, a synthesis, but institutionally serious. For a $15 federal minimum by 2025, CBO's 2019 median estimate was 1.3 million jobs lost (a 0.8% reduction in employment) against 17 million workers getting raises. The 2021 update was 1.4 million. CBO's own uncertainty bands are wide — "about zero to 3.7 million" in 2019 — which is itself informative: even the federal scorekeeper does not pretend to know the employment effect precisely. ([CBO 2019](https://www.cbo.gov/publication/55410), [CBO 2021](https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2021-02/56975-Minimum-Wage.pdf))

## Verdict, calibrated

- **"Modest minimum wage increases cause massive unemployment" is wrong** as stated. The evidence for "massive" effects from modest increases is thin. You are right to be skeptical of that claim.
- **"Small or null aggregate employment effects for modest increases" is a defensible summary** of a large and methodologically strong slice of the modern literature — *especially* for the kind of state-level increases studied in Cengiz et al. You are reading that correctly.
- **But "small or null" understates what the best adversarial evidence shows.** Specifically: (a) hours can adjust even when headcount doesn't (Jardim et al.); (b) effects for directly-affected, less-experienced, and teen workers are consistently more negative than aggregate effects (Neumark & Shirley; Clemens & Wither); and (c) the effects get larger as the minimum wage bites deeper into the local wage distribution — a $15 federal minimum in Mississippi is a very different object than a $15 minimum in Seattle, and the literature on the former does not exist yet because that policy has never been tried.
- **Where I'd calibrate the empirical question:** for minimum wage increases that keep the ratio of minimum-to-median wage below roughly 0.55–0.60, aggregate employment effects look small. Above that — into territory the US has little historical experience with — the evidence is genuinely thin and extrapolation from the modest-increase literature is speculative, not empirical.

So: you're right, but the people arguing with you are not entirely wrong — they're often making a narrower claim (teens, hours, directly affected workers, very large increases) than the "massive unemployment" version you correctly dismiss. If you want to be maximally defensible, the sentence is: *"Modest minimum wage increases in the US have had small or null aggregate employment effects, with real but moderate negative effects concentrated on hours and on young/less-experienced workers."* That sentence survives contact with both camps.

## What would change my mind

- A well-identified study of a $15+ federal minimum actually implemented across the full US wage distribution (including low-median-wage states), showing employment effects clearly in either direction. This policy has not happened, so the literature can't tell us what it would do.
- A replicated bunching-estimator result that specifically tracks *hours* (not just jobs) across many state-level increases and still finds null effects — this would weaken the Jardim/Seattle adversarial finding.
- A new meta-analysis that resolves the Cengiz-vs-Neumark methodological disagreement by testing both approaches on the same data.

## Fallacies / frames to watch for on this topic

- **Motte-and-bailey on both sides**: "minimum wages don't cost jobs" (bailey) vs. "modest minimum wage increases in the US have had small aggregate employment effects" (motte); "minimum wages cause unemployment" (bailey) vs. "very large minimum wages relative to local wages reduce hours for less-experienced workers" (motte). When you see either strong claim, check which one the speaker will retreat to under pressure.
- **Ecological fallacy**: aggregate employment null does not imply that no specific group lost jobs or hours. It often doesn't.
- **Extrapolation out of sample**: most of the modern literature studies increases to ratios the US has seen before. A $15 federal minimum in 2025 would push ratios in low-wage states past any historical US data point. "The evidence shows small effects" and "we know what would happen" are not the same sentence.
