Broadly, yes — your reading is defensible and reflects where a large share of the empirical literature has landed over the past ~30 years. But it's worth being precise about *what* the evidence shows and where the genuine disagreements still are, because "small or null effects" is often overstated in both directions.

## What the evidence actually shows

**Modest minimum wage increases tend to have small employment effects.**

The modern empirical era starts with Card and Krueger's 1994 New Jersey/Pennsylvania fast-food study, which found no negative employment effect from NJ's wage hike. That paper was controversial, but the methodological approach — comparing similar labor markets across policy borders — spawned a large follow-on literature. Key landmarks:

- **Dube, Lester, and Reich (2010)** extended the border-discontinuity design to all contiguous U.S. county pairs and found no significant employment effects in low-wage sectors.
- **Cengiz, Dube, Lindner, and Zipperer (2019, QJE)** used a "bunching" estimator across 138 state-level minimum wage changes. They found that jobs paying below the new minimum disappeared, but an almost equal number appeared just above it — i.e., wages rose with little net job loss. This is probably the most influential recent paper.
- **Seattle's $13/$15 hike** produced dueling studies: the University of Washington team (Jardim et al.) found hours reductions large enough to lower low-wage workers' earnings; the Berkeley team (Reich et al.) found the standard small effects. The UW result is the strongest recent counter-evidence and is taken seriously, though critics note issues with how multi-site employers were handled in the data.
- **Cross-country evidence** (UK Low Pay Commission research, German minimum wage introduction in 2015) has generally found small disemployment effects, sometimes null.

**Meta-analyses** — Doucouliagos and Stanley (2009), Belman and Wolfson (2014), Dube's 2019 report for the UK government — converge on employment elasticities near zero to about −0.1 to −0.2 for teens and very-low-wage workers, meaning a 10% minimum wage increase reduces employment in those groups by at most 1–2%, and often less. Neumark and Wascher have long argued the true effect is larger (their meta-work lands around −0.1 to −0.3), and theirs is the main dissenting camp.

## Important caveats

1. **"Modest" is doing real work in your sentence.** The consensus is strongest for increases that keep the minimum below roughly 55–60% of the local median wage (the "Kaitz ratio"). Push far above that and the evidence gets thinner and more worrying. A federal $15 in rural Mississippi would be well outside the range most studies cover.

2. **Null average effects can mask distributional effects.** Hours reductions, changes in non-wage compensation, shifts in who gets hired (more experienced workers displacing less experienced ones), automation on longer time horizons, and reduced job growth rather than outright layoffs are all harder to detect and may be real.

3. **Publication bias and researcher priors matter.** Both sides have accused the other of specification searching. Dube vs. Neumark is a genuine, decades-long methodological disagreement, not just noise.

4. **The theoretical story has updated.** The old textbook "minimum wage = unemployment" story assumes competitive labor markets. Monopsony models (where employers have wage-setting power) predict that modest minimum wages can *raise* employment, and there's growing evidence that low-wage labor markets have meaningful monopsony power. This is why modern economists are less dogmatic than in the 1980s.

## Bottom line

If someone claims a modest minimum wage increase will cause "massive unemployment," that is not what the mainstream empirical literature shows — you're right to be skeptical. The best evidence suggests small, often statistically insignificant disemployment effects for increases within the historical range, with wage gains for low-wage workers that outweigh the employment costs on average.

But "small or null" isn't the same as "zero and therefore free." There are real tradeoffs, they likely grow nonlinearly with the size of the increase relative to local wages, and reasonable economists disagree about magnitudes. The honest summary is: the pro-minimum-wage side has won the "does it cause mass unemployment?" debate; the unresolved question is how high you can push it before the costs start mattering.

Good starting points if you want to dig in: Dube's 2019 report for the UK government (very readable), the Cengiz et al. 2019 QJE paper, and Neumark and Wascher's 2008 book *Minimum Wages* for the strongest version of the skeptical case.
