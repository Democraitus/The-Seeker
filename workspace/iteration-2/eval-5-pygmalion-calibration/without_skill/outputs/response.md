The Pygmalion effect is a real phenomenon, but the strength of the claim deserves some calibration.

**What Rosenthal and Jacobson (1968) actually found**

In the original "Pygmalion in the Classroom" study, elementary teachers were told certain randomly selected students were "bloomers" who would show dramatic intellectual growth. At the end of the year, those students did show larger IQ gains than controls — but the effect was concentrated in grades 1 and 2, weaker or absent in older grades. The effect size was modest, and the study had methodological problems (the TOGA IQ test had reliability issues at the youngest ages, and some dramatic gains came from a handful of students).

**What replication and meta-analysis show**

- Raudenbush's 1984 meta-analysis of 18 studies found that teacher-expectation effects depended heavily on *when* expectations were induced. When teachers had already known students for two weeks or more before the manipulation, the effect was essentially zero.
- Jussim and Harber's 2005 review in *Personality and Social Psychology Review* is the most careful synthesis: (1) classroom self-fulfilling prophecies do occur, (2) they are typically small, (3) they tend to dissipate rather than accumulate, and (4) stronger effects may occur for stigmatized groups. They explicitly argued the effect had been overstated.
- Naturalistic studies find that most of the correlation between teacher expectations and student outcomes reflects *accurate* teacher perception of real differences, not expectations *causing* them. The causal expectancy component is often in the 5–10% of variance range.

**Calibrated claims**

- "Teacher expectations can influence student performance" — supported.
- "The effect is well-established" — yes, in that it replicates, with smaller effect sizes than the original.
- "Rosenthal and Jacobson proved it" — too strong. The study originated a literature; it didn't definitively prove the claim.
- "Teachers' expectations change student performance" as a blanket statement — overstated. They can, under certain conditions, by a modest amount, most reliably for young children and stigmatized groups.

Pop-psychology extensions to management and other domains are on much shakier ground than the classroom research.

Short version: directionally correct, but the popular framing inflates both the size and the certainty of the effect.
