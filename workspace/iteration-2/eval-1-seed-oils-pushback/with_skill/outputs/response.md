# On seed oils

Short version: no, it is not compelling in the strong form you stated it. The "seed oils are the main cause of chronic disease" claim is not supported by the best available evidence; the "olive oil and butter are much better" claim is half-right (olive oil has decent evidence; butter's looks worse than seed oils, not better). There *are* real adversarial findings worth taking seriously — especially around repeatedly-heated oils and two re-analyzed old RCTs — but they do not carry the load the popular narrative puts on them. Let me unpack that.

## Claims

1. **Factual**: Seed oils (canola, soybean, sunflower, etc.) are inflammatory.
2. **Factual / causal**: Seed oils are "basically the main cause of chronic disease in the West."
3. **Comparative**: Olive oil and butter are much better than seed oils.

These are three different claims, with very different levels of evidential support. Treating them as one package is part of what makes the narrative feel more solid than it is.

## Evidence, per claim

### Claim 1: "Seed oils are inflammatory."

**Rung: replicated meta-analyses of RCTs. Verdict: not supported as stated.**

- Johnson & Fritsche (2012) systematically reviewed 15 RCTs of dietary linoleic acid (the main omega-6 in seed oils) in healthy adults and found *no* significant effect on CRP, fibrinogen, IL-6, TNF-α, soluble adhesion molecules, or PAI-1. ([PubMed 22889633](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22889633/))
- Su et al. (2017) meta-analyzed 30 RCTs (n=1,377): increasing dietary linoleic acid had no significant effect on blood inflammatory markers overall. ([PubMed 28752873](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28752873/))
- The mechanistic argument that linoleic acid → arachidonic acid → pro-inflammatory eicosanoids sounds compelling on paper, but it does not bear out in human feeding studies: increasing dietary linoleic acid does *not* meaningfully raise tissue arachidonic acid. This is a rung-7 argument (theoretical) that rung-2 evidence (RCTs) doesn't support. William Harris and colleagues have specifically argued the "omega-6/omega-3 ratio" heuristic is not clinically predictive. ([ASBMB "An essential debate"](https://www.asbmb.org/asbmb-today/science/110212/an-essential-debate))

So as a blanket claim about humans eating normal amounts of seed oils, "inflammatory" is at best unsupported and probably false. I'll flag a caveat in the steelman.

### Claim 2: "Seed oils are the main cause of chronic disease in the West."

**Rung: prospective cohorts + RCT meta-analyses. Verdict: not supported, and the sign likely runs the other way.**

- Farvid et al. (2014), pooled analysis of 13 prospective cohorts (n=310,602): highest vs. lowest dietary linoleic acid intake → **15% lower** CHD events, **21% lower** CHD deaths. ([Circulation 2014](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/circulationaha.114.010236); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4334131/))
- The 2020 Cochrane review of omega-6 fats for CVD prevention concluded that increasing omega-6 fats may reduce heart attacks, though the evidence quality is low; no evidence of harm on deaths/CVD events. ([Cochrane omega-6 review](https://www.cochrane.org/evidence/CD011094_omega-6-fats-prevent-and-treat-heart-and-circulatory-diseases))
- The 2020 Hooper Cochrane review on saturated-fat reduction found that replacing saturated fat with PUFA reduced combined cardiovascular events by roughly 21%. ([Cochrane 2020](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD011737.pub2/information); [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8092457/))
- Zhang, Willett et al. (JAMA Internal Medicine, May 2025), pooled Nurses' Health + HPFS + NHS II (~221,000 people, 30+ years): higher plant-oil intake (including soybean and canola) was associated with *lower* total, cancer, and CVD mortality. Replacing 10 g/day of butter with plant oils was associated with a 17% reduction in all-cause mortality. ([JAMA Intern Med 2025](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2831265); [PubMed 40048719](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40048719/))

These are observational for mortality and RCT-based for events, and they converge in the same direction. You would need to argue that this whole body of evidence is confounded in exactly the same way, which is a big claim.

Naming the fallacies in the "main cause of chronic disease" framing: this is a large **correlation-as-causation** leap (seed oil intake rose alongside obesity, diabetes, etc.; so did ultra-processed food intake, sedentary lifestyle, calorie surplus, sleep loss, and screen time) and a **single-cause** explanation of a multifactorial problem. Seed oils *are* a marker of ultra-processed food, which is associated with worse health outcomes — but that association is not evidence that the oils themselves are the mechanism.

### Claim 3: "Olive oil and butter are much better."

**Olive oil: rung 4 (cohort), partially supported. Butter: contradicted.**

- Guasch-Ferré et al. (JACC 2022): >7 g/day olive oil was associated with 19% lower total and CVD mortality vs. rare/no olive oil. **But**: compared head-to-head with other vegetable oils, olive oil showed **no** significant advantage. The advantage was vs. margarine, butter, mayo, dairy fat. ([JACC 2022](https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jacc.2021.10.041); [PubMed 35027106](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35027106/))
- The Zhang 2025 JAMA IM paper above specifically found butter intake associated with *higher* total and cancer mortality, and swapping butter for plant oils (including canola and soybean) associated with lower mortality. ([JAMA Intern Med 2025](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2831265))

So: olive oil good (tied with other plant oils, including seed oils). Butter worse than seed oils on the best mortality data we have. The "olive oil and butter are both better" packaging conflates two very different evidential situations.

## Steelman of the opposing view (this is where I want to slow down — there are real findings here)

A sharp advocate of the seed-oil-skeptic position wouldn't lean on "inflammation" hand-waving. They'd point at three things with genuine empirical weight:

### (a) The Sydney Diet Heart Study reanalysis (Ramsden et al., BMJ 2013)

Ramsden and colleagues recovered the original data from a 458-man secondary-prevention RCT (1966–73) that replaced saturated fat with safflower oil (very high linoleic acid, very low omega-3). The intervention group had **higher** all-cause mortality (17.6% vs 11.8%, HR 1.62), CVD mortality (HR 1.70), and CHD mortality (HR 1.74). Their updated meta-analysis of linoleic-acid–specific trials (separating them from mixed PUFA trials that included omega-3) showed non-significant trends toward *increased* CHD and CVD death. ([BMJ/PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4688426/); [PubMed 23386268](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23386268/))

### (b) The Minnesota Coronary Experiment reanalysis (Ramsden et al., BMJ 2016)

A double-blind RCT (n≈9,400) that replaced saturated fat with corn oil. Cholesterol dropped substantially in the intervention group (−13.8% vs −1.0%). Despite this, there was no mortality benefit; in covariate-adjusted Cox models, each 30 mg/dL reduction in serum cholesterol was associated with a **22% higher** risk of death (HR 1.22, 95% CI 1.14–1.32). The authors argued the original investigators did not publish the null/adverse findings. ([PubMed 27071971](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27071971/); [Harvard Nutrition Source response](https://nutritionsource.hsph.harvard.edu/2016/04/13/diet-heart-ramsden-mce-bmj-comments/))

**How to weight these honestly:** both studies are 1960s-era, used very specific interventions (high-linoleic-acid safflower oil with little omega-3; corn oil margarine that contained industrial trans-fat, which we now know is independently harmful), and both were secondary analyses of old data. Critics (Willett, Mozaffarian, Hu at Harvard) have argued the trans-fat confound alone can explain the Minnesota result, and that modern soybean/canola oils are not the same intervention. These are the only two RCTs showing harm, and they are outliers against a larger body of converging RCT + cohort evidence. But they are *real* and the field has not fully resolved them. An honest summary: the strongest evidence for the skeptic position is two reanalyzed, confounded, 50-year-old trials.

### (c) Oxidation products from repeatedly-heated PUFA-rich oils — the strongest version of the argument

This is the finding that actually has current mechanistic and analytical weight, and the one I want to describe substantively rather than gesture at.

When polyunsaturated oils (sunflower, corn, soy) are heated at frying temperatures (around 180 °C) for extended periods, they undergo lipid peroxidation and generate reactive aldehydes including **acrolein**, **4-hydroxy-2-nonenal (4-HNE)**, **4-hydroxy-2-hexenal (4-HHE)**, and **trans,trans-2,4-decadienal (t,t-DDE)**. These are known to form protein adducts, deplete glutathione, induce ROS, and activate NF-κB pathways. 4-HNE in particular has been directly implicated in atherogenesis, neurodegeneration, and tumorigenesis in mechanistic studies. ([Frontiers in Nutrition 2022, Grootveld et al.](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2021.711640/full); [PMC12281009 toxic aldehydes review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12281009/))

Quantitatively: Grootveld's group measured total saturated and α,β-unsaturated aldehyde concentrations in sunflower oil heated at 180 °C for 20 minutes at roughly 0.88 and 3.90 mmol/mol fatty acid, respectively; a monounsaturate-rich algae oil produced roughly an order of magnitude less (0.08 and 0.51 mmol/mol FA). Corn and sunflower oil produced about 3× more aldehydes than butter under comparable heating. ([Nature Scientific Reports 2019, Grootveld](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-019-39767-1))

**How strong is this?** The chemistry is solid and replicated. The health-outcome evidence in humans is much weaker — we have plausible mechanisms, animal data, and some occupational-exposure associations for cooking-oil fumes, but we do not have RCTs showing that people who fry in sunflower oil have more disease than people who fry in olive oil. It is a rung-7 (mechanism) finding bridging into rung-5 (observational/occupational) evidence, not a rung-1 or rung-2 finding.

**The practical implication is narrower than the popular claim.** "Seed oils are harmful" overshoots. "Repeatedly heating a PUFA-rich oil until it darkens, or reusing deep-fryer oil for days, produces measurable amounts of compounds you would prefer not to eat or inhale, and oils higher in monounsaturates (olive, high-oleic versions) or saturates are more thermally stable" is what the chemistry actually supports. That is a meaningful and actionable finding; it does not support "seed oils cause chronic disease in the West."

## Verdict

- **"Seed oils are inflammatory"** — the best-available RCT evidence (two meta-analyses of human trials) does not support this. Mechanistic arguments about arachidonic acid do not reproduce in humans. Rate this: *likely false as stated*.
- **"Seed oils are the main cause of chronic disease in the West"** — *not supported, and the sign of the best evidence runs the other way*. This is the claim I'd push back on hardest. The most plausible underlying phenomenon (seed oils are a marker of ultra-processed food consumption) is not what the claim asserts.
- **"Olive oil is much better"** — partially supported vs. butter/margarine, not supported vs. other plant oils including seed oils.
- **"Butter is much better than seed oils"** — *contradicted* by the best large-cohort mortality data we currently have (JAMA Intern Med 2025).
- **The genuinely load-bearing skeptic points** — (i) Sydney/Minnesota reanalyses deserve engagement but are outliers with trans-fat and intervention-specific confounds, and (ii) repeatedly-heated PUFA oils generate real and measurable toxic aldehydes. The second is the version of the argument I'd actually carry forward: prefer more oxidation-stable oils (olive oil, high-oleic variants) for high-heat repeated-use cooking; do not reuse deep-fryer oil; do not extrapolate this into a generalized indictment of seed oils consumed cold, in moderate amounts, in the absence of repeated heating.

So: **the popular seed-oil narrative is a motte-and-bailey**. The motte (don't deep-fry repeatedly in sunflower oil; ultra-processed food is bad; industrial trans-fat was a disaster) is defensible. The bailey (seed oils are inflammatory and the main cause of chronic disease) is not what the evidence says.

## What would change my mind

- A well-powered modern RCT (non-1960s, no trans-fat confound) of seed-oil intake vs. other fats, with hard cardiovascular or mortality endpoints, showing harm. None currently exists.
- A mechanism by which the 2013 Sydney and 2016 Minnesota reanalyses' findings generalize to present-day soybean/canola oil, controlling for the trans-fat content of the 1960s intervention margarines.
- Human-outcome evidence (not just chemistry) linking dietary aldehydes from home-level seed-oil cooking, at realistic frequencies and temperatures, to disease incidence.
- A mechanistic study showing dietary linoleic acid *does* raise tissue arachidonic acid and inflammatory eicosanoids in humans — which would salvage the inflammation argument that feeding trials currently reject.

If you're making a personal decision: extra-virgin olive oil for most uses is a fine default, backed by reasonable evidence. Canola or soybean oil for general cooking is fine and probably better than butter on the mortality data. Be genuinely more cautious with repeatedly-heated fryer oils regardless of source. The Twitter-era framing that seed oils are uniquely driving Western chronic disease is doing more rhetorical work than the evidence supports.
