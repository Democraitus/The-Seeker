Honestly, no — it's a compelling *story*, but the evidence is much weaker and messier than the popular narrative suggests. Let me walk through where it goes wrong.

**The core claim to interrogate: "seed oils cause chronic disease"**

The mechanistic argument usually goes: seed oils are high in linoleic acid (an omega-6 polyunsaturated fat), omega-6s produce arachidonic acid, arachidonic acid produces inflammatory eicosanoids, therefore seed oils = inflammation = disease. Each arrow in that chain is either oversimplified or wrong:

1. **Linoleic acid doesn't reliably raise inflammatory markers in humans.** A 2017 systematic review in *Nutrients* (Johnson & Fritsche) looked at 15 randomized trials and found that increasing dietary linoleic acid had essentially no effect on CRP, IL-6, TNF-alpha, or other inflammatory markers. The "omega-6 is inflammatory" idea comes largely from cell culture and rodent studies that don't translate.

2. **Conversion of linoleic acid to arachidonic acid in humans is very low** — typically under 0.5%. Tissue arachidonic acid levels barely budge when you change linoleic acid intake.

3. **Epidemiology generally shows the opposite of what the seed-oil-is-bad theory predicts.** Higher blood/tissue levels of linoleic acid are associated with *lower* cardiovascular mortality, lower type 2 diabetes risk, and lower all-cause mortality in large pooled analyses (e.g., Marklund et al. 2019 in *Circulation*, pooling 30 cohorts).

4. **Randomized trials of replacing saturated fat with polyunsaturated fat (including seed oils) generally reduce cardiovascular events** — this is the basis of guidelines, not just a hunch. The Cochrane review on reducing saturated fat (Hooper et al.) finds a ~17% reduction in cardiovascular events.

**What about the "chronic disease rose alongside seed oil consumption" argument?**
This is the strongest-sounding piece of the case and the weakest on inspection. Obesity, sugar intake, ultra-processed food consumption, sedentary lifestyles, smoking patterns, and calorie availability all changed dramatically over the same period. Correlation across secular trends is nearly worthless for causal inference. The specific culprit of obesity epidemiology is total calories and ultra-processed food, not the oil per se.

**The kernel of truth**
- Deep-fried fast food cooked in repeatedly reused oil produces oxidation products and is genuinely not great — but that's a cooking/processing issue, not an inherent property of the oil.
- Diets extremely skewed toward omega-6 with very little omega-3 probably aren't optimal. Getting some omega-3s (fish, walnuts, flax) matters.
- "Ultra-processed food is bad for you" is well-supported. Seed oils happen to be in a lot of ultra-processed food, so they get blamed for effects that track with the processing, the calorie density, and the refined carbs they're paired with.

**Olive oil and butter**
- Olive oil (especially extra virgin) genuinely does look good in trials — PREDIMED is the headline study. No argument there.
- Butter is not the villain it was once made out to be, but it's not clearly better than canola or soybean oil either. Meta-analyses (Pimpin et al. 2016) find butter roughly neutral for cardiovascular disease, slightly worse than unsaturated oils.

**Bottom line**
The seed oil narrative takes a real nugget (ultra-processed food is harmful, omega-3 balance matters, reused frying oil is bad) and inflates it into a grand unified theory of chronic disease that the randomized and prospective evidence doesn't support. If you like olive oil, use olive oil — it's great. But avoiding canola in your stir-fry isn't going to meaningfully change your health, and the influencers pushing this framing are generally confident well beyond what the data warrants.

The useful questions are: how much ultra-processed food am I eating, am I getting any omega-3s, and am I in a reasonable calorie range? Those matter far more than which bottle of oil is on your counter.
